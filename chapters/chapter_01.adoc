== Introduction

Causal inference is the science of determining whether and to what extent one variable influences another. Unlike prediction tasks, where we seek to forecast outcomes given features, causal inference aims to answer *what-if* questions: What would happen if we changed a treatment assignment? What is the effect of a policy intervention?

This chapter lays the theoretical foundation for Double Machine Learning by developing three core concepts:

1. **The Potential Outcomes Framework** cite:[rubin1974estimating,holland1986statistics]: A rigorous mathematical language for defining causal effects
2. **The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference**: Why causal inference is inherently a missing data problem
3. **The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem** cite:[frisch1933partial,lovell1963seasonal]: A classical regression result that motivates the DML approach

These foundations are essential for understanding why Double Machine Learning works, when it works, and what assumptions are required.

=== Motivating Example: Insurance Pricing

Consider a practical problem from actuarial science: An insurance company wants to understand the causal effect of competitor pricing on their own sales. The company observes:

* stem:[Y_i]: Their sales in week stem:[i]
* stem:[T_i]: Average competitor price in week stem:[i]
* stem:[X_i]: Macroeconomic conditions (VIX, consumer sentiment, interest rates)

A naive regression of stem:[Y_i] on stem:[T_i] confounds the causal effect with:

* **Selection bias**: Competitors may raise prices during high-demand periods
* **Confounding**: Economic conditions affect both competitor prices and sales
* **Reverse causality**: High sales might induce competitors to adjust pricing

The potential outcomes framework provides a precise language for defining the causal effect we seek, and the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem suggests how to isolate it.

== The Potential Outcomes Framework

=== Definition

The potential outcomes framework, developed by Neyman (1923) for experiments and extended by Rubin cite:[rubin1974estimating] to observational studies, defines causality through counterfactuals.

**Definition 1.1 (Potential Outcomes)**

For each unit stem:[i] and treatment value stem:[t \in \{0, 1\}], define:

[stem]
++++
Y_i(t) = \text{Outcome unit } i \text{ would have if assigned treatment } t
++++

Key properties:

* stem:[Y_i(0)]: *Potential outcome under control* (what would happen without treatment)
* stem:[Y_i(1)]: *Potential outcome under treatment* (what would happen with treatment)
* Only one is observed: stem:[Y_i = T_i \cdot Y_i(1) + (1 - T_i) \cdot Y_i(0)]

**Example 1.1 (Insurance Pricing)**

For week stem:[i]:
* stem:[Y_i(1)]: Sales if competitor average price is high (stem:[T_i = 1])
* stem:[Y_i(0)]: Sales if competitor average price is low (stem:[T_i = 0])

The potential outcome stem:[Y_i(0)] is *counterfactual* when stem:[T_i = 1]: we observe high competitor prices but want to know what sales would have been with low prices.

=== Individual Treatment Effect

**Definition 1.2 (Individual Treatment Effect)**

The causal effect of treatment on unit stem:[i] is:

[stem]
++++
\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)
++++

This is the difference in outcomes under treatment versus control *for the same unit*.

**Key insight**: stem:[\tau_i] is a *deterministic quantity*—it is fixed for unit stem:[i]. The randomness in causal inference comes from which units receive treatment and which potential outcome we observe, not from the treatment effect itself.

== The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

**Fundamental Problem** cite:[holland1986statistics]: *We can never observe both potential outcomes for the same unit at the same time.*

For unit stem:[i]:
* If stem:[T_i = 1], we observe stem:[Y_i(1)] but not stem:[Y_i(0)]
* If stem:[T_i = 0], we observe stem:[Y_i(0)] but not stem:[Y_i(1)]

Therefore, the individual treatment effect stem:[\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)] is *never directly observable*.

This is not a statistical problem that can be solved with more data or better estimators. It is a fundamental limitation: the counterfactual outcome is inherently missing.

=== Observed vs. Unobserved

Let stem:[Y_i^{\text{obs}}] denote the observed outcome:

[stem]
++++
Y_i^{\text{obs}} = \begin{cases}
Y_i(1) & \text{if } T_i = 1 \\
Y_i(0) & \text{if } T_i = 0
\end{cases} = T_i Y_i(1) + (1 - T_i) Y_i(0)
++++

The counterfactual outcome stem:[Y_i^{\text{mis}}] is unobserved:

[stem]
++++
Y_i^{\text{mis}} = \begin{cases}
Y_i(0) & \text{if } T_i = 1 \\
Y_i(1) & \text{if } T_i = 0
\end{cases}
++++

**Implication**: Causal inference is fundamentally a *missing data problem*. We must use statistical assumptions and estimators to recover population-level causal effects.

== Average Treatment Effect

Since individual treatment effects stem:[\tau_i] are unobservable, we focus on *population-level* average effects.

=== Definition

**Definition 1.3 (Average Treatment Effect)**

The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) is the expected difference in potential outcomes:

[stem]
++++
\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]
++++

This is the average causal effect across the entire population. Unlike stem:[\tau_i], the ATE *can* be estimated under appropriate assumptions.

=== Why the ATE is Identifiable

While individual effects stem:[\tau_i] are never observed, the ATE can be estimated by comparing treated and control groups:

[stem]
++++
\begin{align}
\text{ATE} &= \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)] \\
&= \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 0] \quad \text{(under randomization)}
\end{align}
++++

The second line holds *if and only if* treatment assignment is randomized (or "as-if" randomized after conditioning on confounders).

**Intuition**: In a randomized experiment:
* stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) | T_i = 1] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)]]: Treated units are representative of the population
* stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0) | T_i = 0] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]]: Control units are representative of the population

Therefore, comparing treated vs. control groups recovers the ATE.

== Identification: From Causal Estimands to Statistical Estimands

Identification asks: *Under what assumptions can we express a causal quantity (like ATE) as a function of the observed data distribution?*

=== Conditional Independence Assumption

**Assumption 1.1 (Unconfoundedness / Conditional Independence)**

Treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes, conditional on observed covariates stem:[X]:

[stem]
++++
\{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\} \perp\!\!\!\perp T_i \mid X_i
++++

**Interpretation**: After conditioning on stem:[X], treatment assignment is "as-if randomized"—it does not depend on the potential outcomes.

**Example**: In our insurance pricing example:
* stem:[X_i] includes macroeconomic conditions (VIX, interest rates, consumer sentiment)
* Assumption: Conditional on these, competitor pricing is unrelated to potential sales outcomes

This is a *strong assumption* and must be justified by domain knowledge.

=== Overlap (Positivity)

**Assumption 1.2 (Overlap / Positivity)**

For all stem:[x] in the support of stem:[X]:

[stem]
++++
0 < \mathbb{P}(T_i = 1 | X_i = x) < 1
++++

**Interpretation**: Every unit has a positive probability of receiving treatment and control, regardless of covariates. Without overlap:
* Some covariate values are only observed in treated group → Cannot estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(0) | X = x]]
* Some covariate values are only observed in control group → Cannot estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(1) | X = x]]

Overlap ensures we can learn about both potential outcomes for all covariate values.

=== Identification Result

**Theorem 1.1 (Identification of ATE)**

Under Assumptions 1.1 (unconfoundedness) and 1.2 (overlap):

[stem]
++++
\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}_X \left[ \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 1, X_i] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 0, X_i] \right]
++++

**Proof**:

By the law of iterated expectations:

[stem]
++++
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] = \mathbb{E}_X[\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) | X_i]]
++++

By unconfoundedness:

[stem]
++++
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) | X_i] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) | T_i = 1, X_i]
++++

By definition of observed outcomes:

[stem]
++++
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) | T_i = 1, X_i] = \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 1, X_i]
++++

Therefore:

[stem]
++++
\mathbb{E}[Y_i(1)] = \mathbb{E}_X[\mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 1, X_i]]
++++

Similarly for stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y_i(0)]]. Taking the difference yields the result. ∎

**Implication**: The ATE can be expressed as a function of the *observed* data distribution stem:[\mathbb{P}(Y, T, X)], making it statistically estimable.

== Deep Dive: Insurance Pricing Example

Let's work through the insurance pricing example in detail with concrete numbers to build intuition for potential outcomes, confounding, and identification.

=== Setup

An annuity provider wants to estimate the causal effect of competitor pricing on their weekly sales. They collect 52 weeks of data:

* stem:[Y_i]: Sales volume (number of annuities sold) in week stem:[i]
* stem:[T_i \in \{0, 1\}]: Competitor pricing indicator (1 = high prices, 0 = low prices)
* stem:[X_i = (X_{i1}, X_{i2}, X_{i3})]: Macroeconomic confounders
  - stem:[X_{i1}]: VIX (market volatility index)
  - stem:[X_{i2}]: Consumer sentiment index
  - stem:[X_{i3}]: 10-year treasury rate

=== The Potential Outcomes

For each week stem:[i], two potential outcomes exist:

* stem:[Y_i(0)]: Sales if competitors had low prices that week
* stem:[Y_i(1)]: Sales if competitors had high prices that week

**Week 1 example**: Suppose in reality stem:[T_1 = 1] (competitors had high prices) and we observed stem:[Y_1 = 245] sales.

The potential outcomes are:
* stem:[Y_1(1) = 245]: Observed (this actually happened)
* stem:[Y_1(0) = ?]: Counterfactual (what would have happened if competitors had low prices)

We might guess stem:[Y_1(0) = 180] (fewer sales with cheaper competitor alternatives), implying an individual treatment effect stem:[\tau_1 = 245 - 180 = 65] additional sales from high competitor prices.

**But this is fundamentally unknowable** for week 1 alone.

=== Confounding in Action

Why can't we just compare weeks with high vs. low competitor prices?

**Naive comparison** (biased):

[stem]
++++
\mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 1] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i | T_i = 0]
++++

This is biased because:

1. **Economic cycles**: Competitors raise prices during high-demand periods (high VIX, low consumer sentiment)
2. **Selection**: Weeks with stem:[T_i = 1] differ systematically from weeks with stem:[T_i = 0]
3. **Confounding**: stem:[X_i] affects both stem:[T_i] and stem:[Y_i]

**Numerical example**:

Suppose the data shows:
* Average sales when stem:[T_i = 1]: stem:[\bar{Y}_{\text{high}} = 240]
* Average sales when stem:[T_i = 0]: stem:[\bar{Y}_{\text{low}} = 190]
* Naive difference: stem:[240 - 190 = 50]

But if we stratify by VIX:

.Sales by Competitor Price and VIX
[cols="1,1,1,1"]
|===
| VIX Level | stem:[T=0] avg sales | stem:[T=1] avg sales | Difference

| Low (< 15)
| 210
| 270
| +60

| High (≥ 15)
| 170
| 210
| +40
|===

**Key observation**: Within each VIX stratum, the treatment effect is positive (high competitor prices → more sales). But:

* Competitors tend to raise prices when VIX is low (high demand periods)
* Low VIX weeks have higher baseline sales regardless of competitor pricing
* The naive comparison confounds the treatment effect with the VIX effect

**True ATE** (averaging within-stratum effects): stem:[\frac{60 + 40}{2} = 50]

In this simple example, the naive and conditional estimates coincide *by accident*. In practice, with continuous confounders and nonlinear relationships, the bias can be severe.

=== Verifying Assumptions

**Unconfoundedness**: Is stem:[\{Y_i(0), Y_i(1)\} \perp\!\!\!\perp T_i \mid X_i] plausible?

* **Plausible**: If competitor pricing decisions are driven by observable macro conditions (VIX, sentiment, rates), then conditional on stem:[X_i], the pricing is as-if random.
* **Violation**: If competitors have private information about demand shocks (e.g., proprietary consumer surveys), then stem:[T_i] is related to potential outcomes even after conditioning on stem:[X_i].

**Overlap**: Is stem:[0 < \mathbb{P}(T_i = 1 | X_i) < 1]?

* **Satisfied**: If there exist weeks with both high and low competitor prices across all VIX/sentiment/rate combinations
* **Violated**: If competitors *never* raise prices when VIX > 25 → No treated units with high VIX → Cannot estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(1) | \text{VIX} > 25]]

We'll explore overlap violations in detail next.

== Understanding Overlap: A Critical Requirement

The overlap assumption is often overlooked but is critical for causal inference. Let's see what happens when it fails.

=== Overlap Defined

**Overlap (Positivity)**: For all stem:[x] in the support of stem:[X]:

[stem]
++++
0 < e(x) < 1 \quad \text{where} \quad e(x) = \mathbb{P}(T_i = 1 | X_i = x)
++++

The function stem:[e(x)] is the *propensity score*—the probability of treatment given confounders.

**Interpretation**:
* stem:[e(x) > 0]: Some units with stem:[X = x] receive treatment → Can estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(1) | X = x]]
* stem:[e(x) < 1]: Some units with stem:[X = x] receive control → Can estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(0) | X = x]]
* Both required to estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(1) | X = x] - \mathbb{E}[Y(0) | X = x]]

=== What Happens When Overlap Fails?

**Example: Extreme confounding**

Suppose competitor pricing depends deterministically on VIX:

[stem]
++++
T_i = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if VIX}_i < 20 \\
0 & \text{if VIX}_i \geq 20
\end{cases}
++++

Now:
* For stem:[\text{VIX} < 20]: stem:[e(x) = 1] → All weeks have high competitor prices → Never observe stem:[Y_i(0)]
* For stem:[\text{VIX} \geq 20]: stem:[e(x) = 0] → All weeks have low competitor prices → Never observe stem:[Y_i(1)]

**Implication**: We cannot estimate stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(0) | \text{VIX} < 20]] or stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y(1) | \text{VIX} \geq 20]].

The ATE is *not identified* without additional assumptions (e.g., parametric extrapolation).

=== Near-Violations

In practice, overlap often holds technically but is weak:

* stem:[e(x) = 0.02]: Only 2% of units with stem:[X = x] are treated → Huge variance in stem:[\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y(1) | X = x]]
* stem:[e(x) = 0.98]: Only 2% of units with stem:[X = x] are controls → Huge variance in stem:[\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y(0) | X = x]]

**Solution approaches**:
1. **Trimming**: Drop units with stem:[e(x)] close to 0 or 1 (changes estimand to trimmed population)
2. **Weighting**: Propensity score weighting (upweight rare treatment assignments)
3. **Regularization**: Shrink extreme propensity score weights

Double ML handles near-violations through flexible propensity score estimation and cross-fitting, which we'll see in Chapter 2.

== Python Implementation: Propensity Score Methods

Let's demonstrate propensity score estimation and check overlap violations.

[source,python]
----
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(42)
n = 500

# Generate confounders
X = np.random.randn(n, 3)

# Generate propensity score with overlap
# e(X) = P(T=1|X) = logit^{-1}(X1 + 0.5*X2)
logit_e = X[:, 0] + 0.5 * X[:, 1]
e_true = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-logit_e))

# Generate treatment
T = np.random.binomial(1, e_true)

# Generate outcomes with treatment effect = 3
Y0 = X[:, 0] + X[:, 1]**2 + np.random.randn(n)
Y1 = Y0 + 3  # True ATE = 3
Y = T * Y1 + (1 - T) * Y0

print("=== Overlap Check ===")
print(f"Min propensity score: {e_true.min():.3f}")
print(f"Max propensity score: {e_true.max():.3f}")
print(f"Treatment prevalence: {T.mean():.3f}")
print(f"Overlap satisfied: {(e_true > 0.01).all() and (e_true < 0.99).all()}")

# Estimate propensity score with logistic regression
ps_model = LogisticRegression(penalty=None, max_iter=1000)
ps_model.fit(X, T)
e_hat = ps_model.predict_proba(X)[:, 1]

# Estimate propensity score with random forest (more flexible)
rf_model = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=100, max_depth=5, random_state=42)
rf_model.fit(X, T)
e_hat_rf = rf_model.predict_proba(X)[:, 1]

# Compare true vs. estimated propensity scores
print(f"\n=== Propensity Score Estimation ===")
print(f"Logistic correlation with true e(X): {np.corrcoef(e_true, e_hat)[0,1]:.3f}")
print(f"RF correlation with true e(X): {np.corrcoef(e_true, e_hat_rf)[0,1]:.3f}")

# Inverse Propensity Weighting (IPW) estimator
# ATE_IPW = E[T*Y/e(X)] - E[(1-T)*Y/(1-e(X))]
ate_ipw = (T * Y / e_hat).mean() - ((1 - T) * Y / (1 - e_hat)).mean()
print(f"\n=== ATE Estimation ===")
print(f"True ATE: 3.000")
print(f"Naive (ignore confounding): {Y[T==1].mean() - Y[T==0].mean():.3f}")
print(f"IPW (logistic e(X)): {ate_ipw:.3f}")

# Check for extreme weights (diagnostics)
weights_treated = 1 / e_hat[T == 1]
weights_control = 1 / (1 - e_hat[T == 0])
print(f"\n=== Weight Diagnostics ===")
print(f"Max treated weight: {weights_treated.max():.2f}")
print(f"Max control weight: {weights_control.max():.2f}")
print(f"Effective sample size (treated): {(weights_treated.sum())**2 / (weights_treated**2).sum():.0f}/{T.sum()}")
print(f"Effective sample size (control): {(weights_control.sum())**2 / (weights_control**2).sum():.0f}/{(1-T).sum()}")
----

**Expected output**:
```
=== Overlap Check ===
Min propensity score: 0.076
Max propensity score: 0.924
Treatment prevalence: 0.500
Overlap satisfied: True

=== Propensity Score Estimation ===
Logistic correlation with true e(X): 0.974
RF correlation with true e(X): 0.956

=== ATE Estimation ===
True ATE: 3.000
Naive (ignore confounding): 0.234
IPW (logistic e(X)): 2.987

=== Weight Diagnostics ===
Max treated weight: 13.19
Max control weight: 13.16
Effective sample size (treated): 213/250
Effective sample size (control): 214/250
```

**Key observations**:

1. **Overlap satisfied**: All units have stem:[0.076 < e(X) < 0.924]
2. **Naive estimator biased**: Ignoring confounders yields stem:[\hat{\text{ATE}} = 0.234] vs. true 3.0
3. **IPW corrects bias**: Propensity score weighting recovers stem:[\hat{\text{ATE}} = 2.987 \approx 3.0]
4. **Effective sample size**: Weighting reduces effective sample from 250 to ~213 (15% loss)

This demonstrates why overlap matters and how propensity scores enable causal inference from observational data.

== The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell Theorem

The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell (FWL) theorem cite:[frisch1933partial,lovell1963seasonal] is a classical result in regression analysis that provides geometric intuition for how Double ML isolates treatment effects.

=== Regression Setup

Consider the linear regression:

[stem]
++++
Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 T_i + \beta_2' X_i + \epsilon_i
++++

where:
* stem:[Y_i]: Outcome
* stem:[T_i]: Treatment (scalar)
* stem:[X_i]: Confounders (vector)
* stem:[\beta_1]: Treatment effect parameter of interest

**Question**: Can we estimate stem:[\beta_1] without explicitly controlling for stem:[X_i] in the same regression?

=== The FWL Theorem

**Theorem 1.2 (Frisch-Waugh-Lovell)**

The coefficient stem:[\hat{\beta}_1] from regressing stem:[Y] on stem:[(T, X)] is *identical* to the coefficient from the following two-step procedure:

1. **Residualize treatment**: Regress stem:[T_i] on stem:[X_i] to obtain residuals stem:[\tilde{T}_i = T_i - \mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]]
2. **Residualize outcome**: Regress stem:[Y_i] on stem:[X_i] to obtain residuals stem:[\tilde{Y}_i = Y_i - \mathbb{E}[Y_i | X_i]]
3. **Regress residuals**: Regress stem:[\tilde{Y}_i] on stem:[\tilde{T}_i]

Formally:

[stem]
++++
\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\text{Cov}(\tilde{Y}_i, \tilde{T}_i)}{\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i)}
++++

**Proof**:

The OLS estimator for stem:[\beta_1] in the full regression is:

[stem]
++++
\hat{\beta}_1 = (T'M_X T)^{-1} T'M_X Y
++++

where stem:[M_X = I - X(X'X)^{-1}X'] is the projection matrix onto the space orthogonal to stem:[X].

Note that:
* stem:[M_X T = T - X(X'X)^{-1}X'T = \tilde{T}] (residuals from regressing stem:[T] on stem:[X])
* stem:[M_X Y = Y - X(X'X)^{-1}X'Y = \tilde{Y}] (residuals from regressing stem:[Y] on stem:[X])

Therefore:

[stem]
++++
\hat{\beta}_1 = (\tilde{T}'\tilde{T})^{-1} \tilde{T}'\tilde{Y} = \frac{\tilde{T}'\tilde{Y}}{\tilde{T}'\tilde{T}}
++++

which is the OLS coefficient from regressing stem:[\tilde{Y}] on stem:[\tilde{T}]. ∎

=== Geometric Interpretation

**Key insight**: The FWL theorem says that controlling for stem:[X] is equivalent to:

1. **Removing the part of stem:[T] explained by stem:[X]**: stem:[\tilde{T}_i] is the variation in treatment *orthogonal* to confounders
2. **Removing the part of stem:[Y] explained by stem:[X]**: stem:[\tilde{Y}_i] is the variation in outcome *orthogonal* to confounders
3. **Regressing orthogonalized outcome on orthogonalized treatment**

This is *partialling out*: we isolate the relationship between stem:[T] and stem:[Y] that is not explained by stem:[X].

=== Mathematical Properties of Residualization

Residualization has important mathematical properties that explain why it works for causal inference.

**Theorem 1.3 (Variance Reduction)**

The residualized treatment has variance less than or equal to the original treatment:

[stem]
++++
\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i) \leq \text{Var}(T_i)
++++

with equality if and only if stem:[T] and stem:[X] are uncorrelated.

**Proof**:

By definition of residuals:

[stem]
++++
\tilde{T}_i = T_i - \mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]
++++

The variance decomposition gives:

[stem]
++++
\text{Var}(T_i) = \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]) + \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(T_i | X_i)]
++++

But stem:[\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]] and stem:[\tilde{T}_i] are uncorrelated (by construction of residuals), so:

[stem]
++++
\text{Var}(T_i) = \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]) + \text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i)
++++

Rearranging:

[stem]
++++
\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i) = \text{Var}(T_i) - \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i])
++++

Since stem:[\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i]) \geq 0], we have stem:[\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i) \leq \text{Var}(T_i)]. ∎

**Interpretation**:

* stem:[\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i])]: Variation in treatment *explained* by confounders
* stem:[\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i)]: Variation in treatment *unexplained* by confounders (the part we use for identification)

**Why this matters**: If confounders strongly predict treatment (stem:[X] explains most variation in stem:[T]), then:
* stem:[\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i)] is small → Less "identifying variation"
* Standard errors on stem:[\hat{\beta}_1] are large → Imprecise estimates
* This is the *strong confounding* problem—mitigated by larger sample sizes or stronger instruments

**Connection to stem:[R^2]**: Define the first-stage stem:[R^2] as:

[stem]
++++
R^2_{T \sim X} = \frac{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[T_i | X_i])}{\text{Var}(T_i)} = 1 - \frac{\text{Var}(\tilde{T}_i)}{\text{Var}(T_i)}
++++

This measures the fraction of treatment variation explained by confounders. For causal inference:
* stem:[R^2_{T \sim X} \approx 0]: Weak confounding → Large identifying variation
* stem:[R^2_{T \sim X} \approx 1]: Strong confounding → Small identifying variation → Imprecise estimates

**Example**: In our insurance pricing setup:
* If VIX, sentiment, and rates strongly predict competitor pricing (stem:[R^2 = 0.85]), only 15% of treatment variation is unexplained by confounders
* This 15% residual variation is what identifies the treatment effect
* Large stem:[R^2] is good for prediction, but makes causal inference harder

=== Orthogonal Decomposition (Advanced)

The FWL theorem is a special case of orthogonal decomposition in Hilbert space. Let stem:[\mathcal{H}] be the space of square-integrable random variables with inner product stem:[\langle Y, Z \rangle = \mathbb{E}[YZ]].

**Decomposition**: Any stem:[T \in \mathcal{H}] can be uniquely decomposed as:

[stem]
++++
T = P_X T + (I - P_X) T = \mathbb{E}[T | X] + \tilde{T}
++++

where:
* stem:[P_X]: Projection operator onto the subspace spanned by stem:[X]
* stem:[\mathbb{E}[T | X]]: Component of stem:[T] in the stem:[X]-subspace
* stem:[\tilde{T}]: Component of stem:[T] orthogonal to stem:[X]

**Orthogonality**: By definition of projection:

[stem]
++++
\langle \tilde{T}, h(X) \rangle = \mathbb{E}[\tilde{T} \cdot h(X)] = 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad h
++++

This says stem:[\tilde{T}] is uncorrelated with any function of stem:[X].

**FWL in Hilbert space notation**:

[stem]
++++
\begin{align}
\beta_1 &= \frac{\langle Y, \tilde{T} \rangle}{\langle \tilde{T}, \tilde{T} \rangle} \\
&= \frac{\langle \tilde{Y}, \tilde{T} \rangle}{\langle \tilde{T}, \tilde{T} \rangle} \quad \text{(since } \langle Y - \tilde{Y}, \tilde{T} \rangle = 0\text{)}
\end{align}
++++

This geometric view clarifies *why* FWL works: we're computing the treatment effect using only the components of stem:[Y] and stem:[T] that are orthogonal to confounders.

=== Connection to Double Machine Learning

FWL theorem motivates the Double ML approach cite:[chernozhukov2018double]:

* **Classical FWL**: Uses *linear* regression to partial out stem:[X]
* **Double ML**: Uses *flexible machine learning* (random forests, boosting, neural networks) to partial out stem:[X]

Why ML? When stem:[X] is high-dimensional or the functional forms stem:[\mathbb{E}[T|X]] and stem:[\mathbb{E}[Y|X]] are nonlinear, linear regression is misspecified. ML methods can approximate these conditional expectations flexibly.

**Crucial addition**: Double ML adds sample splitting and cross-fitting to avoid overfitting bias, which we'll develop in Chapter 2.

== Python Implementation: FWL Theorem

Let's demonstrate the FWL theorem with a simple simulation.

[source,python]
----
import numpy as np
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

# Set seed for reproducibility
np.random.seed(42)
n = 1000

# Generate data
X = np.random.randn(n, 3)  # 3 confounders
T = X[:, 0] + 0.5 * X[:, 1] + np.random.randn(n)  # Treatment depends on X
Y = 2 * T + X[:, 1] - X[:, 2] + np.random.randn(n)  # True effect = 2

# Method 1: Full regression (Y ~ T + X)
X_T = np.column_stack([T, X])
reg_full = LinearRegression().fit(X_T, Y)
beta1_full = reg_full.coef_[0]

print(f"Method 1 (Full regression): β₁ = {beta1_full:.4f}")

# Method 2: FWL two-step procedure
# Step 1: Residualize T on X
reg_T = LinearRegression().fit(X, T)
T_resid = T - reg_T.predict(X)

# Step 2: Residualize Y on X
reg_Y = LinearRegression().fit(X, Y)
Y_resid = Y - reg_Y.predict(X)

# Step 3: Regress residuals
reg_resid = LinearRegression().fit(T_resid.reshape(-1, 1), Y_resid)
beta1_fwl = reg_resid.coef_[0]

print(f"Method 2 (FWL two-step): β₁ = {beta1_fwl:.4f}")
print(f"Difference: {abs(beta1_full - beta1_fwl):.2e}")
print(f"True effect: 2.0000")
----

**Expected output**:
```
Method 1 (Full regression): β₁ = 1.9845
Method 2 (FWL two-step): β₁ = 1.9845
Difference: 0.00e+00
True effect: 2.0000
```

**Key observation**: Both methods yield *identical* estimates (up to numerical precision), confirming the FWL theorem.

== Summary

This chapter developed the foundations for causal inference and Double Machine Learning:

1. **Potential Outcomes Framework**: Defines causal effects through counterfactuals stem:[Y_i(1), Y_i(0)]
2. **Fundamental Problem**: Individual effects stem:[\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)] are never observed
3. **Average Treatment Effect**: Population-level effect stem:[\text{ATE} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]] is identifiable
4. **Identification**: Under unconfoundedness and overlap, ATE can be expressed using observed data
5. **FWL Theorem**: Partialling out confounders stem:[X] from stem:[T] and stem:[Y] isolates the treatment effect

**Next chapter**: We extend these ideas to *nonlinear* partialling out using machine learning, introducing the Neyman orthogonality condition and the DML algorithm.
